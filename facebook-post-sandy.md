Every single day in America, millions of families are handed a piece of paper that changes everything — and nobody explains what it means.

Lab results. MRI reports. Discharge summaries. Pages of numbers, abbreviations, and medical jargon that might as well be written in a foreign language.

Your doctor has 7 minutes with you. Your specialist sees 30 patients a day. Your pharmacist is behind a counter with a line out the door.

And you're sitting at a kitchen table at 11 o'clock at night — scared, alone, holding a piece of paper about someone you love — with nobody to ask.

I know that feeling. Most of us do.

Here's what makes it worse: the information you need EXISTS. It's right there in those reports. But it's locked behind a wall of medical language that was never designed for the people it affects most.

That's not an accident. That's a system that was built for doctors and hospitals — not for families.

And it costs lives.

---

I'm a baby boomer. I've sat in those waiting rooms. I've nodded along to explanations I didn't understand because I was too overwhelmed to admit I was lost.

Then last week something happened that I can't stop thinking about.

A woman I'll call Sandra — her husband is 84, a Navy veteran, two years out from major cancer surgery — had been following every instruction the hospital gave her. Every two hours, she flushed his feeding tube with water. Just like they told her.

What nobody told Sandra: plain water dilutes sodium.

Her husband's sodium had crashed to 124. The safe range is 136–145. Dangerously low. The VA sent a letter. Nursing called about an ER evaluation.

Sandra was doing everything right. And nobody connected the dots.

When I sat down and looked at his full history alongside his lab results — the surgery, the feeding tube, the bile he was losing every day, his Navy service on a submarine 60 years ago — the picture became clear in minutes.

The formula wasn't replacing what his body was losing. The flush protocol was working against him. And there was a question about his lung fluid that nobody had thought to ask — tied directly to something that happened six decades ago.

Sandra walked away with five questions she never knew she needed.

His care changed.

She said something that I keep coming back to:

"I finally feel like I have an advocate."
