Why We Built This

She was doing everything right.
Nobody told her it wasn't enough.

Sandy's husband Duane — 84 years old, a Navy veteran, two years out from esophageal cancer surgery — had his sodium crash to 124. The normal range is 136–145. The VA sent a letter. Nursing called about an ER evaluation.

Sandy had been following her instructions perfectly. Every two hours, she flushed his feeding tube with 150ml of plain water. Just like she was told. What nobody told her: plain water dilutes sodium. She was giving him 1,800ml of sodium-diluting water every single day — on top of the bile he was already losing.

When we looked at his labs alongside his full medical history — the surgery, the J-tube formula, the bile loss, his Navy service on a submarine in the 1960s — the picture became clear immediately. His formula wasn't replacing what he was losing. The flush protocol was making it worse. And nobody had ever asked about the asbestos exposure that might explain the fluid in his lungs.

Sandy walked into those conversations with five questions she didn't know she needed to ask. Her husband's care changed.
